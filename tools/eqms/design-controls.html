01 Development Plan
The up-front document that defines your project scope, milestones, team structure, and how you'll execute every other section below. Think of it as the contract between your team and your future QMS auditor.
Why it matters
FDA reviewers use this to assess whether your design process was actually controlled — not improvised. It's the first document pulled in a 510(k) submission.
Common pitfall
Writing a plan after the project is already underway. The plan must be written and approved before design work begins.
7 phases defined · Approved Rev A Open Dev Plan
02 User Needs
Captures what patients, clinicians, and operators actually need the device to do — in plain language, before any engineering decisions. These become the top-level requirements everything else traces back to.
Why it matters
This is what a 510(k) reviewer reads first. If your device solves a problem you've never formally documented, that's a submission risk. Every design input must trace to at least one user need.
Common pitfall
Writing needs as engineering specs ("sensor accuracy ±2%") instead of user statements ("clinicians must be alerted within one minute"). Save the numbers for Design Inputs.

03 Design Inputs
Translates each user need into a measurable, testable engineering requirement. These are the specs your engineers build to — and the criteria your V&V team will test against later.
Why it matters
If a design input isn't testable, you can't verify it — and if you can't verify it, you can't close out 820.30(f). Incomplete inputs are the #1 reason V&V test plans fall apart.
Common pitfall
Leaving inputs in "Draft" indefinitely. Unapproved inputs aren't controlled — any change during development could go undetected and break your traceability.
7 inputs · 5 approved, 2 in review Open Design Inputs
04 Design Outputs
The actual deliverables your team produces — drawings, firmware, test methods, labeling, manufacturing specs. Each output must trace to at least one design input, demonstrating it was built for a specific reason.
Why it matters
Outputs form the Device Master Record (DMR) — the technical package that transfers to manufacturing. Any output that can't be traced to an input is a gap the FDA will flag.
Common pitfall
Treating labeling and Instructions for Use as an afterthought. They're design outputs and must trace to user needs and safety inputs — not just marketing.

05 Design Reviews
Formal checkpoints where a multidisciplinary team reviews whether the design is ready to move to the next phase. Required at key milestones by 820.30(e) — not optional, and not just a team meeting.
Why it matters
Design reviews catch issues before they become expensive. An FDA inspector will look at meeting minutes and action item closure — they need to show independent reviewers were present.
Common pitfall
Having only engineers in the room. A valid design review requires at least one reviewer who was not responsible for the design being reviewed.
2 reviews · 1 approved, 1 open Open Design Reviews
06 Verification & Validation
Two distinct activities: Verification ("did we build it right?" — design outputs meet design inputs) and Validation ("did we build the right thing?" — the device meets user needs in the real-world environment).
Why it matters
This is the proof package. A 510(k) or PMA without complete V&V test reports will not clear review. Every design input must have a corresponding verification test that passed.
Common pitfall
Conflating verification and validation — or running validation first. You must verify the design meets its inputs before validating with real users in a realistic environment.
2 activities · 1 in progress, 1 draft Open V&V
Continuous Traceability Matrix
A living cross-reference that maps every user need to a design input, a design output, and a V&V result. You maintain it throughout the process — adding rows as each section progresses, not assembling it at the end. Any incomplete row is an open regulatory gap.
Why it matters
This is the single most common document auditors request during an FDA inspection. A complete, gap-free trace matrix is the clearest possible signal that your design process was controlled.
Common pitfall
Building the trace matrix after the fact, at submission time. It should be maintained continuously — gaps caught early cost hours to fix; gaps found at submission cost months.

GUIDANCE Design Controls Overview
Design controls are the structured process of translating what patients and clinicians need into verified, validated device requirements — then proving you met them. They're required for all Class II and III devices under 21 CFR 820.30 and ISO 13485 §7.3.
Six sequential sections form an unbroken chain — each feeds the next. The Traceability Matrix runs alongside throughout, confirming every link is in place. Gaps in the chain become 483 observations.
The Chain
User Needs → Design Inputs → Design Outputs → Design Reviews → V&V — all cross-referenced in the Trace Matrix.
The majority of device recalls trace back to failures in this process: requirements that were never written down, outputs that didn't trace to inputs, or V&V that didn't cover what patients actually do.
