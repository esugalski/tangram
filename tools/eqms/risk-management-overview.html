01 Risk Management Plan
The foundational controlled document that defines how risk management will be executed throughout the device lifecycle. It sets the scope, identifies the qualified personnel responsible, establishes risk acceptability criteria, and specifies the risk management activities to be performed — all before analysis begins.
Why it matters
Acceptability criteria must be defined before you evaluate your risks — not after you've seen the results. An FDA reviewer or notified body auditor will check that your criteria are grounded in state-of-the-art evidence, not adjusted to make your risk matrix look clean.
Common pitfall
Setting acceptance criteria that aren't linked to clinical evidence or relevant guidance. "Score ≤ 6 is acceptable" is meaningless without defining what each severity and probability level represents for your specific patient population.

02 Hazard Identification
A systematic process of identifying all foreseeable hazards associated with the device during normal use, reasonably foreseeable misuse, and fault conditions. For each hazard, you trace the sequence of events that leads to a hazardous situation, and then to the potential harm — establishing the full causal chain required by ISO 14971.
Why it matters
You can only control risks you've identified. A hazard missed here is an uncontrolled risk in the field. ISO 14971 requires consideration of all foreseeable uses, all patient populations, all environmental conditions, and all reasonably foreseeable failure modes.
Common pitfall
Confusing a hazard with a hazardous situation. "Electrical energy" is a hazard. "Patient contact with a live conductor during normal use" is the hazardous situation. ISO 14971 requires both — the distinction drives how you structure your controls.

03 Risk Analysis
For each hazardous situation, estimates the probability of harm occurring and the severity of that harm to produce an initial risk estimate. Where risks exceed the acceptance criteria, risk controls are applied using the prescribed hierarchy: inherent safety by design first, then protective measures, then information for safety. Residual risk is re-evaluated after each control and verified to confirm effectiveness.
Why it matters
Risk controls must be verified — a control that isn't confirmed to work doesn't reduce risk. ISO 14971 also requires you to check whether each control introduces new hazards. Every risk reduction claim needs an evidence trail.
Common pitfall
Jumping straight to controls before completing the initial risk estimate. You need a documented before-and-after comparison to demonstrate risk reduction — and to justify the final residual risk conclusion against your pre-defined criteria.

04 Risk Report
The closing artifact of the risk management file — a summary document that confirms the complete process has been executed as planned. It captures all identified hazards, the controls implemented, the residual risk evaluations, and the overall benefit-risk determination. The report is the evidence that overall residual risk is acceptable before the device enters the market.
Why it matters
This is the primary risk management artifact auditors request during FDA inspections and notified body reviews. It must demonstrate that all risks have been addressed, that no risk was overlooked, and that the overall residual risk conclusion is consistent with the benefit-risk analysis.
Common pitfall
Treating the report as a one-time submission document. ISO 14971 requires the risk management file — and the report — to be maintained throughout the product lifecycle. Post-market data must be fed back in and the overall risk conclusion revisited.

GUIDANCE Risk Management Overview
ISO 14971:2019 is the international standard for the application of risk management to medical devices. It defines a structured, iterative process that runs from initial design through the entire product lifecycle — not just through submission.
The four phases shown here form a closed loop: the plan sets the rules, hazard identification surfaces the risks, risk analysis and control reduces them, and the report confirms the process was completed as intended. Post-market data then feeds back into the file — reopening the loop.
The Risk Control Hierarchy (ISO 14971 §7)
① Inherent safety by design — eliminate the hazard at source
② Protective measures — guards, alarms, interlocks
③ Information for safety — warnings, labeling, IFU
Apply in order. Do not skip to labeling when a design change could eliminate the hazard.
The risk management file is a living collection of all records — plan, hazard analysis, risk evaluations, control verification, and the final report. It is the primary artifact auditors pull during an FDA inspection or notified body review.
